# On the Corruption of Vanguard's Resolve

*From the recovered journal of **Staunton Vhane** — the unmaking of the greatsword Vanguard's Resolve into Breaker of Wills, set down the seventh day of Erastus.*

Brother Joran brought me the greatsword this morning, carried reverently in both hands as if it still deserved such treatment. I recognized it immediately—Vanguard's Resolve, the blade that had been passed down through seven commanders of the Second Shield Wall, each of whom had died defending Drezen's northern approaches. The last to wield it was Commander Rhestine, who fell in the same assault that took Father. Its blade was forged from cold iron blessed in the waters of the Sellen, and along its length ran inscriptions in Celestial that spoke of courage unwavering and duty unbroken. Even sheathed, it emanated an aura that steadied the hearts of those who stood nearby—I had felt it myself, standing in formation as a young knight, drawing strength from its mere presence.

Joran's hands trembled as he held it. "Are you certain, brother?" he asked. "This was Commander Rhestine's—she trusted it to your keeping before—"

"Before she died for nothing," I cut him short. "Before she threw her life away on walls that were meant to fall. Before Iomedae watched another faithful servant bleed out and did nothing." My voice was harsher than I intended, but the truth of it steadied my resolve. "Take it to the forge."

The weapon's power had been legendary among the crusaders. Any commander who bore it found their troops rallying even in the darkest moments—the blade projected an aura of steadfast courage that banished fear and exhaustion within thirty paces. When drawn in battle, it seemed to multiply the bearer's voice, allowing their commands to ring clear across the chaos of combat. And thrice each day, its bearer could invoke its blessing, causing the blade to flash with brilliant light that would inspire nearby allies to acts of heroism beyond their normal capabilities, while striking terror into the hearts of demon-kind.

I had dreamed of wielding it myself, once. Before I understood that dreams built on sand and prayer are worth less than dust.

The corruption forge in the lower depths burns with different light now—black flames fed by despair and fueled by broken faith. Aponavisius herself provided the specifications, her six arms tracing sigils in the air as she described the process with an artist's precision. Three days, she commanded. Three days with the weapon submerged in those unholy fires, quenched each dawn with the blood of a devoted servant of the Inheritor. We found three such servants in our dungeons—crusaders from Rhestine's own company who had refused to renounce their oaths even after months of... persuasion.

The first night, the greatsword screamed. Not a sound, precisely—more a feeling that resonated in the bones, a clarion note of anguish that set even the corrupted guards to whimpering. Joran emerged from the forge chamber pale and shaking, unable to meet my eyes. "It knows what we're doing to it," he whispered. "It's calling for its wielders. All of them. I can hear their names—"

Good. Let it call. Let it learn that no one answers.

By the second day, the cold iron had begun to corrode from within, spreading from the point of contact with the flames like a cancer. The Celestial inscriptions writhed and reformed into Abyssal script that hurt to read—promises of courage became threats of cowardice, oaths of protection became guarantees of betrayal. The steady aura flickered, guttered, died for long moments before flaring back—weaker each time, polluted with an undertone of creeping dread.

On the third dawn, as the final sacrifice's blood was poured across the blade, something within it broke. I felt it from my chambers—a snap like the severing of a great chain that had bound together the will of hundreds, perhaps thousands, of faithful warriors across the decades. The scream that followed was not anguish but fury, the rage of something noble that had been twisted into a shape it was never meant to hold, forced to serve purposes that violated every principle it had been forged to uphold.

When Joran brought it forth, I barely recognized it. The cold iron had become pitted black steel that seemed to drink in the light around it, and the Abyssal inscriptions pulsed with a sickly green luminescence. The aura of steadfast courage was gone, replaced by an emanation of crushing despair—a sphere of supernatural terror that would sap the will to fight from even the bravest warrior. Where once it had amplified a commander's voice to inspire, now it would magnify their cruelest words, carrying threats and condemnations that would break the spirit of subordinates. And the blade's invocation, once used to inspire heroism, now called forth a wave of paralyzing fear that would send former allies fleeing in mindless panic.

I named it Breaker of Wills, for that is precisely what it had become—a weapon to shatter the resolve that we once thought unbreakable.

I carried it myself to the throne room where Aponavisius held court. She reclined upon a seat fashioned from the melted-down bells of Drezen's temples, her serpentine lower body coiled beneath her, her six arms arranged in a pose of languid authority. When I laid the corrupted blade before her, all six of her eyes fixed upon it with an expression I had rarely seen on her face—something approaching satisfaction.

"Exquisite," she purred, lifting the greatsword with two of her hands while the others gestured in approval. The blade seemed to resonate with her presence, its green luminescence brightening. "The irony is... delicious. A weapon meant to unite crusaders, now perfect for ensuring their division." She turned the blade, examining the Abyssal script. "The inscriptions are particularly well done. They promise unity while delivering paranoia, offer strength while instilling weakness."

She rose from her throne in one fluid motion, the blade held before her. "This shall go to General Khorramzadeh. The Storm King has been requesting a weapon suitable for breaking the remaining crusader strongholds in the northern reaches. What better tool than one that turns their own courage into cowardice?" Her smile was terrible to behold. "He will carry it when he marches on the Fourth Shield Wall. Let them see their own legend corrupted. Let them know that even their most sacred relics can fall."

"As you command, Lady Aponavisius," I said, bowing low.

As I turned to leave, one of her hands caught my shoulder—not threatening, but almost gentle. "You have done well, Staunton Vhane. Your forge has proven its worth again." A pause, and then: "Does it please you? To see such symbols of your old faith remade?"

I met her gaze and found, to my surprise, that I could answer honestly. "It does."

She released me, a knowing look in all six eyes. "Good. There are more relics in Kenabres that would benefit from your... artistic touch. In time, perhaps you will work upon the Sword of Valor itself."

In time, I thought as I walked back to my sanctum. Perhaps.

But that night, alone in the darkness, I found myself listening to the silence where the blade's screaming had been. It had sounded, in those final moments, like seven voices crying out in unison—the seven commanders who had wielded it, calling out a warning I had chosen not to hear.

The weapon serves the Abyss well now. That is all that matters.

—S.V.

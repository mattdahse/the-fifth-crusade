# Rabiah's Dream

*The mythic dream that came to **Rabiah** in the wake of the Wardstone's breaking.*

*As you collapse into exhausted sleep after the battle at the Gray Garrison, the birthmark on your belly—the one you've always hidden—begins to tingle and warm...*

You are five years old, sitting in a cramped room in your foster parents' home in Kenabres. Rain patters against the window as you trace the strange spiral pattern on your stomach with small fingers. Your foster mother catches you and quickly pulls your shirt down.

"Never show that to anyone," she whispers, looking nervously toward the door. "There are those who would... take interest."

"What is it?" you ask.

"A legacy," she says, her face softening. "From your real parents."

"Where are they?" you ask, as you have a hundred times before.

She turns away. "They were brave people. They went into the Worldwound on a mission. They never returned."

The scene shifts. You're eight, hiding beneath the stairs as your foster parents argue in hushed, urgent tones.

"They found another one in Nerosyan," your foster father says. "Same mark, same age. They're looking for them all now."

"We have to move her," your foster mother insists. "The agreement was temporary protection, not—"

"The agreement was broken when they never came back! What do you propose? Abandon her to the streets?"

Something cold settles in your chest. That night, you feel the first stirring of magic in your blood—a candle flame that bends toward your outstretched hand, responding to emotions you can't name.

The dream blurs. You're ten, running through the back alleys of Nerosyan. How you got here is a fog—memories of a night journey, whispered goodbyes, promises of safety that rang hollow even then. Your foster parents are gone, whether by choice or force you cannot say.

The streets of Nerosyan become your classroom. You learn to smile at the right strangers, to make yourself useful to merchants who might spare food, to hide when certain patrols pass by. And always, always, to keep the spiral birthmark covered.

At night, alone in whatever shelter you've found, you practice the strange abilities that grow stronger with each passing month. Fire dances between your fingertips. Objects shift slightly when you concentrate. Small predictions come to you in flashes—which market stall will discard food, which guard will look the other way.

The scene shifts again. You're twelve, sitting with an old woman who sells potions from a small cart. She's taken a liking to you, teaches you herbs and simple remedies. One day, she catches a glimpse of your birthmark as you reach high for a jar.

Her eyes widen. "Child," she whispers, "do you know what that is?"

Before you can answer, she traces an identical spiral in the air. "The Seeker's Spiral. My brother wore that mark—he was a Riftwarden."

"What's a Riftwarden?" you ask, the word stirring something deep in your memory.

"Guardians," she says, looking around nervously. "They watch the places where worlds touch, where barriers thin. They ensure nothing... crosses that shouldn't."

In the following weeks, she tells you fragments—never the full story. The Riftwardens are a secret society. They guard against planar threats. They have enemies on both sides of the Worldwound.

"Your parents," she says one day, "if they bore or gave you that mark, they were powerful indeed."

The dream shifts. Years compress into fleeting images—you growing taller, your magic stronger, your network of street contacts wider. You learn to stitch your own clothes, perfecting hidden pockets and concealing layers. Your red braid becomes your signature, a flash of color in the marketplace that friends recognize from afar.

The powers in your blood develop in unpredictable ways. Sometimes you know things you shouldn't possibly know. Sometimes luck bends unreasonably in your favor. You learn to trust these instincts, to follow the pull of destiny even when it makes no logical sense.

Then a dream comes three nights in a row—a vision of Kenabres, the city of your birth, and a celebration called Armasse. Something important waits there, something connected to the spiral on your belly. Against all reason, you join a merchant caravan headed for the crusader city.

Suddenly, you're in a tavern in Kenabres, nursing a cheap ale and wondering why your instincts led you here. The tavern door bursts open, and a massive half-orc staggers in, already deep in his cups. Something about him catches your attention—a quality you can't name but recognize somehow.

Later, when his drunken boasts nearly start a brawl with some off-duty crusaders, you find yourself intervening. A subtle cantrip here, a distracting illusion there, and suddenly the tension dissolves into laughter. The half-orc—Harlock, he introduces himself—buys you a drink in thanks.

"Tomorrow I join the Eagle Watch," he declares proudly. "Come to the ceremony!"

You hadn't planned to attend the Armasse celebration, but again, that pull of destiny tugs at you. "Perhaps I will," you hear yourself say.

The dream blurs into chaotic images—the demon attack, running through darkened tunnels and ruined rubble-strewn streets, finding yourself fighting alongside Harlock and others, somehow knowing you were exactly where you needed to be despite the horror unfolding around you.

Then the Gray Garrison. The wardstone chamber. The defender reeling under the force of your companions' blades and spells while you wait for your moment. The invisibility spell sliding over you like a second skin. The Rod of Cancellation cold in your hand as you dart past demons and cultists, your heart pounding but your steps sure.

The touch of the rod against the wardstone. The surge of energy. The desperate race to escape as power built toward explosion. And then, as the wardstone shattered, something awakening within you, as if your very blood recognized the magical energies being released and reached out to claim them.

Then stillness.

You float in an endless void of silver light. Before you materializes a tall woman in gleaming armor, her face bearing the beautiful scars of countless battles, her eyes holding the wisdom of ages. You recognize her instantly—Iomedae, the Inheritor, goddess of righteous valor.

"Young Rabiah," her voice resonates with quiet power. "Few would have had the courage to do what you did—to approach the captured wardstone, to strike the decisive blow that both saved and transformed its power."

She approaches, and the air around you fills with the scent of clean steel and morning dew.

"The mark you bear has guided you to this moment, though neither you nor those who sought to protect you could have foreseen exactly how." She reaches out, her gauntleted hand hovering just above your birthmark, which pulses with warmth even in this dream realm.

"Thank you for your service in freeing the Gray Garrison and for your role in preventing my wardstone from becoming a weapon against the very crusaders it was meant to protect. The power you felt awakening within you is no accident of circumstance, but the fulfillment of possibility long dormant in your blood."

Her hand moves to touch your forehead, and for a moment, you feel an impossible clarity—as if all the random threads of your life suddenly reveal themselves as part of an intricate pattern.

"The Worldwound's chaos has called forth the latent power within you. What some would call mere sorcery is something far more significant—a destined power tied to the very forces your parents once sought to understand and contain."

She steps back, her form beginning to shimmer with divine light.

"The path ahead will reveal more than I can tell you now. Trust the instincts that have guided you thus far. The spiral always completes its journey, though rarely along the path we expect."

As she fades from view, her final words reach you clearly:

"Remember, daughter of the spiral—destiny is not what happens to you, but what you do with what happens. The power now awakened within you seeks purpose. Give it one worthy of your parents' legacy."

You wake with a gasp, your hand instinctively going to your birthmark, which pulses with warmth beneath your clothing. For the first time in your life, you feel no shame or fear of it—only a growing certainty that the mark is not a burden to hide, but the first line of a story you're only now beginning to read.
